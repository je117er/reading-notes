#### question: George Washington’s speeches were at a 16th grade comprehension level. Lincoln was at a 14th grade. JFK spoke in 13th grade level prose. Then when Clinton was president, his speeches had dropped to 8th grade level. Why did presidential speech become simpler despite mass increase in public education?

> In 1960, only about 40% of American adults over age 25 had completed high school. By 1993, that number surged to over 80%. 
>
> In 1960, only 10% of adults held a four-year college degree. By Clinton's presidency, that number more than doubled to 22%.
>
> Yet JFK spoke at a college educated level, and Clinton dropped DOWN to middle school level.
>
> Why?
>
> Also, when Washington was president, did he speak complex 16th grade language only because he knew the voting pool was wealthy educated property owners? But that won’t explain the decline in speech comprehension grade levels in the 20th century?

#### answer

> The decline in the formality of presidential speeches over time is less a sign of declining intelligence or education and more a reflection of how political communication and American society have changed. Like you point out, speeches by early presidents such as Washington were written at what modern readability formulas classify as a "sixteenth-grade" level, and Abraham Lincoln’s speeches around a "fourteenth-grade level", more modern presidents like Bill Clinton often spoke at roughly an "eighth-grade level."[1] Part of it is just an evolution in society. 18th century leaders and elites felt beholden to the idea that you needed to prove your elitism by talking in as sophisticated of a way as possible. As society evolved and society began to embrace higher levels of democracy, this idea waned. More people had access to education, land requirements to vote were retired by state governments, and more common people slowly started getting involved in government, and thus the communication fundamentally evolved with these trends.
>
> At first glance, it might seem paradoxical considering that the American public only became more educated as time went on. But that also is precisely what explains it... education wasn't just a luxury for the elites, so the elite prose associated with it gradually disappeared. The shift also becomes easier to understand when considering not only the audiences presidents were addressing, but the evolution of media, and the changing nature of political persuasion as well.
>
> In the early years of the United States, political speech was aimed primarily at a relatively small and educated circle since most states only let land owners / tax payers vote. Thus the intended audience for many speeches and writings included lawyers, clergy, plantation owners, other important business owners, as well as the newspaper editors who would communicate their speeches with engaged intellectuals.[2] Formal, highly structured prose influenced by Enlightenment philosophy and classical rhetoric was considered a marker of ones knowledge and credibility. Long sentences, elaborate vocabulary, and abstract philosophical language were expected. At the time, political communication functioned almost like literature... speeches were only read carefully in newspapers and almost never heard directly by the public.[3] As American democracy broadened in scope, however, the audience politicians needed to reach did with it. The expansion of suffrage transformed politics into a mass enterprise.[4] Politicians increasingly needed to communicate with millions of ordinary people from wider and what educational and cultural backgrounds. Clear, direct language became politically advantageous because it allowed messages to resonate across the widest possible audience. A speech that could be understood quickly by nearly everyone became more effective than one admired mainly for its complexity by a few aristocratic elites.
>
> Then running parallel to this social change was technological change— the rise of mass media accelerated this trend. Radio, television, and eventually the internet fundamentally changed how speeches were delivered and consumed[5]. You can understand this today by looking at how social media has yet again changed what political speech yields the best results at the ballot box for candidates.
>
> Earlier presidents were essentially only evaluated on how their words looked in print. Modern presidents are judged heavily on how they sound and appear on screen. I'm sure you've heard about how Nixon won the debate in 1960 among listeners on the radio, but those who watched it on TV favored Kennedy. Not only do their words and delivery matter, but a slew of non verbal communication; how one directs their eyes, maintains posture, and even dresses. It's no wonder that candidates with experience acting like Reagan and Trump have been especially good at capturing the public's imagination.
>
> Of course, the words do matter very much, but Television as a medium especially rewards concise phrasing, emotional clarity, and memorable soundbites, slogans, and jargon. Shorter sentences and simpler wording are easier for listeners to process in real time. A president speaking in the dense, formal prose of the eighteenth century would almost certainly come across as distant, stiff, or overly academic rather than intelligent.
>
> Importantly readability scores themselves need to be examined here, as it's easy to incorrectly assume what they actually measure or assess. Readability formulas most frequently measure factors such as sentence length and "word complexity", but they do not measure more abstract factors like depth of thought, emotional resonance, or rhetorical skill.[6] Some of the most famous political speeches in American history are actually written in relatively simple language. Even going back to Lincoln’s Gettysburg Address: it is celebrated not because it is linguistically complicated, but because it communicates profound ideas with extraordinary clarity and emotional force. But even so, it might have never had this effect if TV was around then as the president was quite ill at the time he made it, and it's brief length was so much shorter than the two hour speech that Edward Everett made directly before it.
>
> I hinted on this already but attitudes toward authority and expertise have also seen drastic change. While overly formal prose was once expected, modern audiences, often distrust language that sounds excessively intellectual, thus the political trope where voters want a candidate who "tells it like it is", regardless of how vague and subjective that idea ultimately is. The pressure to appear authentic is perhaps more pressing than to appear educated, as essentially most of the political class has achieved similar levels of education. As a result, many consciously force a conversational style to resonate with voters in an emotional and cultural sense.
>
> Indeed the irony persists as modern presidents often govern in a society that is far more complex than the one faced by George Washington or even Abraham Lincoln, especially considering how American influence has exploded in the years that came after their lives. The simplification is merely aesthetic though. Ultimately modern presidential speech reflects not a decline in intelligence, but an adaptation to mass democracy and modern media. Clarity, accessibility, and emotional resonance have become more valuable political tools than elaborate prose.
>

#### remarks
- readability =/= quality/reach of thought: readability can measure quantitative aspects of language (sentence length, vocab difficulty), but being much weaker at qualitative ones (depth of thought, emotional resonance, rhetorical skills.)
  - is AI changing this tho? for instance, helps measuring the qualitative aspects; makes producing simple prose more easily, etc
- political comm can be viewed as an optimization prob:

|                      | **pre-suffrage**              | **post-suffrage**                               |
|----------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| audience             | aristocratics                 | mass population                                 |
| medium               | print                         | radio, TV, Internet                             |
| objective            | signaling in-group membership | persuasion at scale                             |
| equilibrium strategy | complex, formal prose         | accessible, clear phrasing, emotional resonance |
- education expansion might've reduced the demand for complex pros in the market for political attention/persuasion.
